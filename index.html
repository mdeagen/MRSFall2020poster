Authoring Interactive and Extensible Visualizations from a Materials Knowledge Graph

Michael Deagen, Ph.D. | University of Vermont

F.MT07.10.05

Michael.Deagen@uvm.edu

My name is Michael Deagen, here to give you an overview of the platform for data visualization we have built on top of the MaterialsMine Knowledge Graph, an open data resource of curated experimental research data.

Knowledge graphs enable a web of data

A knowledge graph database stores data and the links between data in an extensible, machine-readable format. By following semantic web standards, we enable interoperability between MaterialsMine and other publicly available datasets, now and in the future.

Objectives

  • Explore datasets linked by knowledge graph
  • Share information visually and interactively
  • Build collection of FAIR data charts
Our new data visualization platform helps us to explore and share information from our knowledge graph in a human-readable format, while providing authors and viewers of charts with autonomy and transparency.

How would you prepare a meal from scratch?

Market + List + Ingredients + Recipe = Meal

Imagine you have to prepare a meal from scratch. You go to the grocery store with a list and add your ingredients to the cart. When you get home, you prepare those ingredients by following a recipe. After you finish cooking, you serve the meal.

Most charts provide limited or no context for data

Many charts you find in journal articles are like the meal but without a direct link to the source “ingredients” or how they were prepared. With our platform, you can see the full process all the way back to the grocery store.

Our charts are defined by a data query and chart spec

Database + Query + Dataset + Spec = Chart

If you think of the grocery store as a database, your grocery list is the query, or request, for a subset of data from that database. The groceries in your cart are data returned by your query. The recipe is like the chart specification that defines the visual encodings of the data, and the meal is the chart which, by the way, you can specify with interactive elements.

Benefits

  • Embed context of data
  • Charts with interactive elements
  • Real-time updating of chart data
This approach to data visualization enables context for the data, the charts can be specified with interactive elements, and by embedding a data query can be automatically updated if new data are added to the knowledge graph.

Combining SPARQL query language with Vega-Lite chart specification language lets us define custom, interactive charts with minimal lines of code

The query, written in SPARQL, and the chart specification, written in Vega-Lite, are high-level abstractions that let you create charts with minimal lines of code. Low-level implementation details are handled automatically by the query engine and chart renderer.

Chart metadata (query, spec, title, description) added to knowledge graph, findable via Charts Gallery

When a chart is published to our platform, it actually becomes part of the knowledge graph and can be accessed through our Charts Gallery along with its metadata including the title, description, SPARQL query, and Vega-Lite specification.
