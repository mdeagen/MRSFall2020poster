Authoring Interactive and Extensible Visualizations from a Materials Knowledge Graph

Michael Deagen, Ph.D. | University of Vermont

F.MT07.10.05

Michael.Deagen@uvm.edu

My name is Michael Deagen, here to give you an overview of the platform for data visualization we have built for the MaterialsMine Knowledge Graph, an open data resource of curated materials research data.

Knowledge graphs enable a web of data

A knowledge graph database stores data and the links between data in an extensible, machine-readable format. By following semantic web standards, we enable interoperability between MaterialsMine and other publicly available datasets, now and in the future.

Objectives

  • Explore datasets linked by knowledge graph
  • Share information visually and interactively
  • Build collection of FAIR data charts
Our new data visualization platform helps us to explore our linked datasets, share information interactively from our knowledge graph, and build a collection of charts that take full advantage of our FAIR data framework.

How would you prepare a meal from scratch?

Market + List + Ingredients + Recipe = Meal

Imagine you have to prepare a meal from scratch. You go to the market with your grocery list and add ingredients to your cart. When you get home, you prepare the ingredients following a recipe, finish cooking, and serve the meal.

Most charts provide limited or no context for data

The problem with many charts you find is that they only provide one view of the data without a direct link to the raw data or how exactly those data were prepared.

Our charts are defined by a data query and chart spec

Database + Query + Dataset + Spec = Chart

With our platform, you can interact with the full process. We accomplish this by treating the market as our database, where the grocery list is the query, or request from the database. The groceries in your cart comprise the dataset returned by your query. The recipe is the chart specification that defines the visual encodings of the data, and the meal is the final chart.

Benefits

  • Embed context of data
  • Charts with interactive elements
  • Real-time updating of chart data
This approach to data visualization enables context for the data, the charts can be specified with interactive elements, and by embedding the data query a chart can be automatically updated if new data are added to the knowledge graph.

Combining SPARQL query language with Vega-Lite chart specification language lets us define custom, interactive charts with minimal lines of code

The query, written in SPARQL, and the chart specification, written in Vega-Lite, are high-level abstractions that let you build charts with minimal lines of code. All the low-level implementation details are handled automatically by the query engine and chart renderer. Just as there are many ways to prepare food from the same ingredients, there are many ways to specify a chart from a given dataset.

Chart metadata (query, spec, title, description) added to knowledge graph, findable in Charts Gallery

When a chart is published to our platform, it actually becomes part of the knowledge graph and can be accessed through our Charts Gallery along with its metadata. With this approach, the knowledge graph grows as users create useful views of the underlying datasets, forming a positive feedback loop.
